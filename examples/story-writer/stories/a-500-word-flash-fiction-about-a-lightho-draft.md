# The Keeper's Tide

The winding crank turns thirty-six times before the lamp catches. I count every one. Dawn comes gray and indifferent, the same as yesterday, the same as the three hundred yesterdays since I arrived. Salt has crusted the lower panes of the lens. I work it free with a rag and mineral oil, circling the glass the way she taught me: clockwise, always clockwise, because the light forgives nothing and neither should the keeper.

The logbook lies open on the desk. *Wind NNW, 12 knots. Visibility poor. Barometer falling.* Her handwriting, confident and slanting, fills every page up to March 14th, where a sentence stops mid-word: *The tide is calling and I think I will…*

I close the book. I don't need to finish the sentence. I've finished it a thousand times in my own voice, and it always ends the same way.

The day passes in rituals: wicks trimmed, brass polished, fuel measured. I don't look at the water. Not yet.

---

At dusk I step onto the catwalk and the wind hits me, wet and heavy, alive with kelp and distance. I grip the rail and force my voice past the stiffness in my throat.

"Shipping lane's clear today," I say. "Three tankers since dawn. Visibility's better than yesterday."

The sea throws itself against the rocks and says nothing back. Foam climbs the basalt in white fingers, retreats. I feel like a fool, a woman talking to a wall of gray.

But the next night I come back. And the next. I tell the sea about the weather, the gulls, the way the light bends through the lens at sunset. The waves keep rhythm beneath my words, *shush, shush, shush*, and it almost sounds like listening.

---

Weeks pass. The tide begins to answer.

First it's a brass button, lodged in a crevice at the base of the rocks, the kind that fastened her coat, the navy one she wore in every storm. I hold it in my palm and feel nothing but the cold, but I put it in my pocket anyway.

The next morning, a page torn from *Moby Dick*, salt-stained, the passage underlined in her pencil: *There is a wisdom that is woe; but there is a woe that is madness.*

Then a stone, smooth and flat, warm as if it had been sitting in someone's hand.

I start talking in confessions now. I tell the sea I should have come sooner. I tell it about the silence after her last letter, how I read the unfinished sentence every night like a prayer that won't complete itself. I tell it about the loneliness, how it has a weight, how it fills the tower from the lamp room down to the cellar where the oil drums sit in the dark.

"You owe me an answer," I say one evening, my voice raw. "You took her."

The waves curl back, retreating, and for a moment the rocks are bare and silent.

---

The storm arrives without warning.

By midnight the wind screams through the catwalk railings and the sea is a wall of black water rising, falling, rising again. I stand at the glass and watch the waves pile against the lighthouse like something trying to get in, or pull me out. Lightning splits the sky and in the flash I see the water's face, and it is not angry. It is open. It is *waiting.*

I press my hand to the glass. "What do you want from me?"

The tower shudders. A wave hits the foundation with a sound like a fist on a door.

"Say it!" I scream. "Say what you did! Say you took her and she let you!"

The storm holds. The wind drops to a moan. The sea goes flat and black and still, an acre of polished obsidian.

Then the foam gathers at the base of the rocks and writes one word in white lace before the next wave erases it:

*Stay.*

I stand there until the glass fogs beneath my hand.

---

Morning. The storm has passed. The rocks are littered with driftwood and shell, and among them something glints: a key. Brass, small, attached to nothing. Not a door key. Not a car key. The kind of key that opens a music box, or a diary, or the lockbox my mother kept beneath her bunk in the keeper's quarters.

I pick it up. I know what it means. A door she left behind. A room in the tower I haven't opened. Stories she locked away because she knew I'd need to find them on my own terms.

I pocket the key beside the button. I don't rush.

---

Night falls clean and cold. I wind the crank, thirty-six turns, and the lamp catches, throwing its beam across the water in a slow, turning sweep. I walk to the catwalk and lean against the rail.

"I brought new bread today," I say. "The bakery in town switched flour suppliers. It's worse."

The sea moves, dark and patient, and the sound it makes against the rocks is different now, softer, almost warm. It sounds like someone settling into a chair beside you.

"The fog's coming in Thursday," I say. "I'll need to check the horn."

*Shush, shush, shush.*

The light turns. The sea answers. And I keep, not out of guilt, not out of promise, but because this is what it looks like when two lonely things decide to stay.
