# What the Tide Remembers

Ellis Varo climbed the spiral stairs at dusk the way he always did — one hand on the cold iron rail, the other cupping the oil tin against his ribs. The steps knew his weight. Eleven years of footsteps had worn shallow crescents into the stone, and he found comfort in that, the way the tower remembered him even on days he forgot himself.

He narrated the sky to the water below as he climbed. "Overcast. Grey on grey on grey. You'd think autumn could at least be dramatic about it."

The sea offered nothing. It seldom did, not in any language Ellis recognized.

He lit the lamp with a match struck against the stone wall, and the great lens caught the flame and spun it into something larger, something that swept the dark water in slow, patient circles. The light said what Ellis could not: *I am here. I am still here.*

He stepped out onto the gallery rail, the wind pressing salt into every fold of his coat. Below, the waves broke against the cliff face in their old, indifferent rhythm. He leaned over the iron bars and spoke.

"I found a gannet this morning. Broken wing, probably a gale that caught it wrong. I set it in the boathouse with some sardines and a dish of water. Don't know if it'll make it. Didn't know if *I'd* make it, that first winter, and look at me now."

He smiled without humor. "Still talking to you."

The sea moved and did not answer. It never did.

Then — a stillness. Not the calm of sheltered water, but a deep, held-breath hush, as though the Atlantic had paused mid-sentence. The waves at the cliff base stopped their churning. The surface smoothed to dark glass. Ellis gripped the rail. He had seen this once before, seven years ago, the night he'd carried the bottle to the edge of the rocks and opened his hand.

The sea went still like a creature deciding whether to be kind.

---

He descended in the dark, lantern in hand, boots finding the cliff path by memory. The tide had retreated further than he'd ever seen it — exposing rock shelves usually claimed by water, pale with barnacles, slick with kelp. Something caught the lantern light at the base of the cliff: a glint, green-white, half-swallowed by seaweed.

Ellis knelt. His fingers closed around glass.

The bottle was intact. Sea-smoothed, the label long dissolved, but he knew its shape — the narrow neck, the slight asymmetry where the glass had been mouth-blown in a shop in Whitby. He had held it on the night of the funeral, Maris's ashes inside like grey powder, fine as flour. He had whispered her name. He had uncorked it and turned it upside down over the waves and watched the ashes scatter like something alive, reaching for the water. And the sea had taken her, as it took everything.

Now the bottle lay empty in his palm. Uncorked. Clean.

The sea had kept what he'd given it. Every grain. It had held Maris in its body for seven years, through storms and tides and winter darkness, and now it returned the vessel. Empty. Returned.

*I held what you gave me,* the stillness seemed to say. *Now hold what I give you.*

Ellis pressed the bottle to his chest and wept — not the muffled, shameful grief of the boathouse, but open and raw, the kind of weeping that comes when a wall you didn't know you'd built finally gives way. The sea waited. It had always been waiting.

---

He climbed back to the lantern room with the bottle in his coat, and set it on the sill beside the lamp. The glass caught the light and threw a small green spectrum across the wall — a color that didn't belong to the lighthouse, to oil, to anything human. It belonged to the deep places, to the dark water where Maris now lived in some form Ellis would never understand but could finally stop trying to describe.

He lit the lamp. He stood at the rail.

The sea below had resumed its motion, but different now — not the mechanical push and pull he'd learned to endure, but something conversational, a rhythm with inflection. He listened. For the first time in eleven years, Ellis Varo stood in the lantern room and did not speak.

The waves turned over in the dark, and the light swept out across the water, and the two of them — the keeper and the sea — kept their vigil together, two old friends who had finally learned that conversation was never the point.

The silence was enough. It had always been enough.
