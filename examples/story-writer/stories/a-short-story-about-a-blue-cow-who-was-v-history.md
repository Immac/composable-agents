# The Cow Who Feared the Sun-Kissed Peel

## The Morning Routine

Azure woke to the smell of hay and old wood, her blue coat catching the first pale light that crept through the barn's open door. She shimmered like a lake at dawn — cerulean from nose to tail, with a sheen that made the other cows look positively dull by comparison. But this morning, as every morning, she was not thinking about her coat. She was thinking about the route.

Three lefts. A right. Then a wide, sweeping arc around the eastern edge of the orchard — the one they called the Sun Lane, though Azure could not think of a worse name for it. She had walked this path every single day for two years, seven months, and a number of mornings she had stopped counting because counting them only made the number worse. The path was safe. The path avoided the orchard. The path kept her alive.

She was certain it kept her alive. The alternative was too awful to consider.

The Incident had happened when she was barely a calf — small enough that a falling banana could have crushed her, or so she told herself. The farmer had been unloading a truck that morning, and Azure had been standing in the wrong place, or perhaps the only place, because the world is full of places and calves do not know which ones to avoid. A single banana, ripe and golden and terrible, had teetered on the edge of the truck bed. It had hung there for one perfect, pregnant moment. Then it fell.

The sound it made when it hit the ground — *thwap* — was, to Azure's memory, indistinguishable from a thunderclap. A wet, heavy, yellow thunderclap that struck the earth three inches from her hoof and left a bruise on her psyche that no amount of clover could heal. She had run. She had not stopped running until she was in the far pasture, panting, staring at the sky as though the sky itself had betrayed her.

She had never trusted yellow fruit again.

"Ho there, Azure!" The voice arrived before the body, which was typical of Sunny, a canary whose smallness was inversely proportional to his volume. He landed on Azure's back with the confidence of a creature who had never once considered that a cow might object to being used as a perch. "Big day today! Big, big day!"

Azure's ears flattened. "Every day is a day, Sunny."

"No, no, no — the farmer brought in crates! Crates upon crates!" Sunny hopped from her left shoulder blade to her right, his yellow feathers catching the barn light in a way that Azure found personally offensive. "Mangoes, Azure! And lemons — oh, the lemons are enormous this year — and golden apples, the kind that shine when you hold them up to the sun. Have you ever held a golden apple to the sun? I haven't. I'm a canary. But I imagine it would be—"

"Which direction did he put them?"

"The orchard, naturally. Where else would you store—"

"Then I'm going the other way." Azure was already moving, hooves clattering against the barn's stone floor. The path. Three lefts. A right. The wide arc.

"Oh, come now," Sunny called after her, launching into the air. "They're just fruit. Round, squishy, perfectly harmless—"

"Goodbye, Sunny."

He followed her anyway, because Sunny always followed her, and because the farm was, as he often said, terribly boring without someone to chase.

The wide arc brought Azure along the stone wall that bordered the orchard, and she kept her eyes forward — always forward — as the branches overhead cast dappled shadows on the path. She did not look left. She did not look into the grove where the lemon trees stood in neat rows, their fruit glowing like small captured suns. She did not look because looking was the first step toward seeing, and seeing was the first step toward remembering, and remembering was the first step toward the cold sweat that made her blue coat turn momentarily, horribly, gray.

The other cows were already in the pasture when she arrived — three of them, brown and white and utterly unbothered, grazing with the placid stupidity that Azure secretly envied. They did not map routes. They did not check for crates. They simply ate.

But today, something was wrong with the pasture.

A wooden crate sat at the entrance, wedged against the gate as though someone had placed it there deliberately. Azure stopped. The crate was open. Inside it, packed in straw like sleeping infants, were lemons — a dozen of them, at least — their yellow skins bright and bumpy and very, very close.

She stood frozen, trembling, one hoof raised mid-step. The other cows continued grazing. A lemon did not threaten them. A lemon was just a lemon to them. But to Azure, that crate was a wall, a gate, a locked door, and beyond it was a pasture she needed but could not reach.

"Well," said Sunny, settling on the fence post. "That is a bit inconvenient, isn't it?"

## The Orchard's Invitation

The farmer made the announcement that evening, his voice carrying across the farm like a brass bell — which was ironic, given that the man could not carry a tune. "Harvest Festival! Saturday! All animals welcome! Path runs through the orchard — every beast gets a walk through the grove and a taste of the first picking!"

The other animals cheered. The goats bleated. The chickens clucked in their competitive way. Azure said nothing.

"The orchard," she repeated, as though the words were a foreign language she was trying to translate and failing. "The *entire* orchard."

Sunny, who had been uncharacteristically quiet, now landed on the fence beside her. "I should mention," he said carefully, "that the path goes right through the lemon grove. Past the mango trees. Under the banana palms. And there will be fruit everywhere — hanging, fallen, piled in baskets. It's rather the whole point of the thing."

Azure stared at him. "You're making this up."

"I am many things, Azure, but a fabricator of festival routes is not among them. I asked the farmer's daughter. She drew me a map." He paused. "I cannot read maps, being a canary, but she described it with great enthusiasm."

The Harvest Festival. It was the one day of the year the farmer brought out the clover cake — real clover cake, not the hay-and-molasses approximation the animals endured the rest of the year, but a proper cake made with fresh clover, honey from the farm's own hives, and whatever unnamed ingredient made Azure's tail wag despite her best efforts to maintain dignity. She had looked forward to it for months. She had *planned* for it, in the way she planned everything — by finding a route that avoided danger.

But there was no route. The festival path was the orchard. The orchard was the festival. The clover cake was in the orchard.

She considered not going. She considered it for a long time, standing at the pasture gate while the sky turned from blue to orange to a deep, bruised purple. But the clover cake called to her, and it was the only thing on earth louder than her fear.

Desperate times, she decided, required desperate wisdom.

The old pear tree stood at the orchard's eastern edge, where the cultivated rows gave way to wild grass and the ground sloped down toward the creek. Bartlett — for that was his name, given to him by a farmer with a sense of humor that had not aged well — was ancient. His trunk was thick as a barrel, his bark deeply furrowed, and his highest branches touched the sky with the tentative reaching of someone who had been trying to leave the ground for a very long time without success.

Azure had spoken to him only twice before, both times at a distance, both times from the safe side of the stone wall. Tonight she crossed the wall. Tonight she stood directly beneath him, close enough to smell the green, woody scent of his leaves.

"Bartlett," she said.

"Azure." His voice was slow, like sap — like honey dripping from a comb on a warm day. "You are closer than you usually stand."

"I need to get through the orchard."

"Yes."

"Not around it. Through it."

"I understand."

"Can you — is there a path? A hidden path? One that avoids the fruit? A path between the trees that a cow could walk without touching a single—"

"There is no such path."

Azure's shoulders sagged. Of course there wasn't. There was never such a path. She had imagined it, the way she imagined a door in her barn that led somewhere other than the barn.

"But," said Bartlett, and the word hung in the air like a pear waiting to fall — though Azure wished he would choose a different metaphor, "you did not come here for a path. You came here because you are afraid, and you are tired of being afraid, and you thought perhaps an old tree might know something about fear."

Azure said nothing, which was confirmation enough.

"I will tell you something," Bartlett continued, his leaves rustling in a breeze that touched nothing else. "When I was young — younger than you are now, if such a thing is possible for a tree — I feared the sky."

Azure blinked. "The sky."

"The sky." Bartlett's branches creaked as he shifted, a sound like old bones settling. "It was so vast. So blue. So absolutely, incomprehensibly *limitless*. I had roots. I had ground. I had the certainty of soil and stone. And above me was this — this *emptiness* that went on forever and asked me to be part of it. I did not want to be part of it. I wanted to stay small. I wanted to stay rooted. I wanted to never grow tall enough to touch the thing I feared."

"What did you do?" Azure asked.

"I grew anyway." A pause. "Not because I stopped being afraid. I grew because I realized that staying small did not make the sky go away. It was always there, above me, patient and enormous, and my fear of it was keeping me from the only thing I was made to do — which was to reach."

Azure stood very still. The evening air was cool against her coat. Somewhere in the orchard, a lemon fell from its branch and landed in the grass with a soft, unremarkable thud.

"You're saying I should walk through."

"I am saying you should stop letting the fear choose your direction. Whether you walk through or not — that is yours to decide. But let it be *yours*."

Azure looked up into Bartlett's branches, and for the first time, she noticed something she had never noticed before: his leaves, catching the last of the twilight, were green — but his fruit, the pears hanging in pairs from every branch, were the palest, softest yellow she had ever seen. Not the sharp yellow of lemons or the screaming yellow of bananas. A quiet yellow. A yellow that asked nothing of her.

She slept poorly that night, dreaming of routes that looped endlessly back to the same place. But sometime before dawn, she rose and walked to the orchard's edge, and she stood there in the moonlight, looking at the lemon tree just inside the gate. Its fruit glowed in the silver light — not bright, not screaming, but luminous, the way a candle is luminous in a dark room. A small, patient light.

For the first time in two years, seven months, and an uncounted number of mornings, Azure thought that the yellow was rather beautiful.

## Through the Grove

The morning of the Harvest Festival dawned golden — which Azure tried not to take personally — and she stood at the orchard gate with her hooves planted and her breath measured. Bartlett's words turned over in her mind like stones in a creek, smooth and worn and insistent.

*Let it be yours.*

She stepped through the gate.

The first lemon tree was five paces in. Azure counted her hoofbeats the way she counted everything — precisely, desperately, as though precision were the same as safety. One. Two. Three. The tree appeared in her peripheral vision, its branches heavy with yellow fruit. She did not look at it directly. Four. Five. Six. She passed it. The lemon hung above her, silent, indifferent, utterly unconcerned with the catastrophe she had imagined. Seven. Eight. Nine. She was past it.

Her heart was hammering, but she was walking.

The mango trees came next — taller, their fruit hanging in clusters like ornaments. The smell hit her before the sight of them: sweet, tropical, overwhelming. She had never been this close to a mango. She had never wanted to be. But the smell was not what she expected. It was not the smell of danger. It was the smell of something ripe and alive and patient, and it clung to the warm air like a lullaby.

She breathed. She counted. She kept moving.

Halfway through the grove, the orchard opened into a small clearing where the farmer had stacked crates for the festival. The lemon trees formed a canopy overhead, and their fruit hung so thick that the light filtering through was yellow-gold, dappled and shifting, the whole world stained with it. Azure stood in that light and realized, with a start, that she was not running. She was standing still. She was breathing.

A flash of yellow darted past her ear.

"Last one to the clearing is a rotten banana!" Sunny shrieked, and he was gone — a yellow streak threading through the yellow branches, disappearing into the grove with the reckless speed of a creature who had never considered that the world might contain things worth fearing.

Azure opened her mouth to call after him. "Sunny, slow—"

The sound that came back was not Sunny's usual chatter. It was a sharp, panicked chirping — the sound of a small creature in sudden, genuine distress.

Azure ran.

She did not think about the lemons. She did not count her hoofbeats. She pushed through the grove with her shoulders low and her blue coat flashing between the yellow branches, and she found Sunny hanging upside-down from a lemon branch by one foot, his wings tangled in a length of twine that some bird-brained squirrel had left strung between the trees. Beneath him, directly beneath him, was a pile of overripe mangoes — soft, squishy, and deep orange-yellow — that would cushion his fall or bury him in sweet, pulpy shame.

"Help!" Sunny squawked. "Help, help, help — I can't get free and the mangoes — Azure, the *mangoes* —"

Azure did not hesitate. She walked forward, into the pile, into the grove, into the yellow. Her hooves sank into the mangoes and the fruit burst beneath her with a sound — soft, wet, absurd. *Squelch.* Not a thunderclap. Not even close. The sound of something yielding, something fragile, something that smelled like sugar and June mornings.

She reached up with her broad blue head and pushed against the branch. It dipped. Sunny released his tangled foot and tumbled onto Azure's neck, clinging to her with both wings and an intensity that suggested he would never let go of anything ever again.

"I've got you," Azure said.

"You're standing in mangoes," Sunny whispered, as though this were a revelation of cosmic significance.

"I know."

"You're *in* them. You're — your hooves are — they're all over your—"

"I know, Sunny."

Sunny looked at her. His usual brightness was gone, replaced by something quieter, something that looked remarkably like shame. "I shouldn't have raced ahead. I wasn't thinking. I forgot that — I forgot you were—" He stopped. "I'm sorry. For all of it. For teasing. For not understanding."

Azure shook mango pulp from her ear. "You're a canary. Understanding is optional."

Sunny laughed — a small, shaky laugh — and nestled closer against her neck. "You walked through. You actually walked through."

"I did."

"And the mangoes—"

"Were soft. And they smelled nice." Azure paused. "Don't tell anyone I said that."

"Not a word."

They walked the rest of the way through the grove together, Sunny perched quietly on Azure's back, and the lemon trees parted around them like a curtain. The orchard gave way to open field, and the open field gave way to the festival, where the clover cake waited and the farmer was already lighting the bonfire and the other animals were singing off-key songs about autumn.

Azure's coat was streaked with mango pulp and lemon juice. She was still blue — bluer, perhaps, for the contrast — and her hooves were sticky and her heart was still beating a little too fast. But she was through. She was on the other side. The clover cake was three paces away, and it was the best thing she had ever tasted, and the orchard behind her was just an orchard — full of lemons and mangoes and bananas and the quiet, unassuming pears that had always, if she was being honest, looked rather like they had something wise to say.

Sunny did not speak for the rest of the afternoon. He sat on Azure's head, his small yellow body warm against her blue crown, and he watched the festival with the wide, still eyes of someone seeing the world for the first time. And when the sun went down and the bonfire painted everything in shades of orange and gold, Sunny leaned forward and pressed his beak gently to the space between Azure's ears.

"Thank you," he said.

And Azure, standing in the golden light with sticky hooves and a full belly and a fear that had not vanished but had, at last, stepped aside — Azure said nothing at all. She simply stayed, and the night was warm, and the fruit trees swayed in the distance, and the world was full of yellow, and she did not mind.

The clover cake was three paces away, and it was the best thing she had ever tasted, and the orchard behind her was just an orchard, full of lemons and mangoes and bananas and the quiet, unassuming pears that had always, if she was being honest, looked rather like they had something wise to say.

Sunny did not speak for the rest of the afternoon. He sat on Azure's head, his small yellow body warm against her blue crown, and he watched the festival with the wide, still eyes of someone seeing the world for the first time. And when the sun went down and the bonfire painted everything in shades of orange and gold, Sunny leaned forward and pressed his beak gently to the space between Azure's ears.

"Thank you," he said.

And Azure, standing in the golden light with sticky hooves and a full belly and a fear that had not vanished but had, at last, stepped aside, Azure said nothing at all. She simply stayed, and the night was warm, and the fruit trees swayed in the distance, and the world was full of yellow, and she did not mind.
