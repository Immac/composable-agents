# The Cloud Hoarder

The Stormspire Peaks rose like crooked fingers clawing at the belly of the sky, and in the highest of them — the one the wind loved most — Veyra kept her hoard.

Not gold. Not jewels. Not the bones of unfortunate knights.

Clouds.

Thousands of them, nested in jars of blown glass and hammered copper, lined along shelves carved from the mountain's own stone. Cumulus clouds sat fat and lazy in wide-mouthed urns. Wisps of morning fog floated in slender vials, barely visible, like breath held in crystal. Nimbus clouds brooded in dark pottery, occasionally grumbling to themselves. Each jar bore a small brass label — the date of capture, the altitude, the color at the moment of sealing.

Veyra moved through her collection now, her great emerald scales whispering against the stone floor, her tail sweeping a slow rhythm that stirred the fog jars into gentle swirls. In her foreclaws she cradled her newest acquisition: a perfect sunset-pink altocumulus, no larger than a fist, roiling softly inside an amber glass bottle. She had chased it for three days across the lowland skies, lunging and cupping it in her claws before it could dissolve into the gathering dark.

"There," she murmured, sliding it onto a shelf between a lavender cirrus and a storm-gray wisp. "Perfect."

The cloud pulsed once — a faint, contented glow — and settled.

Veyra adored that pulse. It meant the cloud was happy. It meant it was safe. It meant it would never drift away, never dissolve, never betray her by vanishing the way beauty always vanished in the open sky.

She was reaching for her labeling tool when the entrance to her cavern shook with a knock — not the percussive rapping of a woodpecker or the polite tapping of a mountain goat, but the frantic, irregular pounding of someone who had been knocking for a very long time and had simply refused to stop.

Veyra's golden eyes narrowed. Visitors were rare. She preferred it that way.

She opened the cavern door a crack, and a gust of wind tumbled in, carrying with it a small, drenched, furious dragon.

He was younger than her by decades — barely more than a hatchling, really — with scales the color of wet slate and eyes like two drops of rain. He was soaked from snout to tail-tip, and he was breathing as though he had flown without rest for days.

"You're Veyra," he gasped. "The Cloud Hoarder."

"I am Veyra," she corrected, drawing herself up to her full and considerable height. "The Cloud Curator, if you please."

"I need a cloud."

"Everyone needs clouds. That is what the sky is for."

"Not just any cloud." He stepped forward, and his voice cracked with a desperation that made the fog jars shiver. "A storm cloud. Small. Dark gray. It rumbled softly, like a heartbeat. It found me when I was a hatchling — no mother, no nest, nothing but rain. It kept me warm. It raised me." His voice dropped. "And then it drifted away, and I've been looking for it ever since."

Veyra stared at him for a long moment. Then she said, very gently, "No."

---

The word hung in the cavern like a stone dropped in still water.

Pip — for that was the name the rain had given him — stared at Veyra with those wet, imploring eyes. "No?"

"I have captured eleven thousand, four hundred and six clouds," Veyra said, turning back to her shelves. "Not one has been returned. I do not make exceptions."

"But this cloud — it's not just vapor to me. It's family."

Veyra's tail stilled. Something flickered behind her eyes — old, buried, sharp as a thorn.

Because she remembered. Not the cloud Pip described, but her own.

---

She had been young once — younger even than Pip — perched on the edge of the Stormspire with her neck craned toward the western horizon, watching the sun melt into the lowlands and set fire to every cloud in its path. A great cumulus had drifted directly overhead, painted in shades of amber and rose and deep, bruised violet, and Veyra had thought: *This is the most beautiful thing I will ever see.*

And then it dissolved.

Not dramatically. Not with thunder or spectacle. It simply thinned, like sugar in rain, and the colors drained away, and the sky was empty, and the moment was gone.

Veyra had sat in the silence that followed and felt something break in her chest — small, quiet, absolute. Beauty had visited her, and she had done nothing to make it stay.

Never again.

She had begun that very night, catching her first cloud — a tiny wisp of fog — in her cupped claws and carrying it, trembling, back to the mountain.

---

The memory faded. Veyra blinked and found Pip still standing before her, rainwater pooling at his feet.

"I understand your grief," she said, and meant it. "But a cloud kept is a cloud saved. If I release them, they dissolve. They vanish. They become nothing."

Pip shook his head slowly. "They become rain. They become mist. They become the next sunset. They don't vanish, Veyra. They *change*."

"That is precisely what I cannot bear."

"Then you've never held one while it was alive."

That landed like a talon in Veyra's breast. She turned away sharply, and her gaze fell on the smallest jar on her shelf — the one she never labeled, never moved, never showed to anyone. Inside it, no larger than a marble, sat a tiny thunderhead. Dark, dense, and grumbling with a sound so low it was more feeling than noise. She had captured it on the worst day of her life, and it had been her favorite ever since — not for its beauty, but for its sadness. It matched her.

She picked it up and held it to her ear.

And felt it.

A pulse. Faint. Slower than before. Like a heartbeat winding down.

The cloud was fading. Even in its glass prison, even shielded from wind and sun, it was losing itself — bit by bit, breath by breath. Captured clouds did not die suddenly. They eroded. They forgot what it felt like to be a storm.

Veyra held the jar against her chest and closed her eyes.

---

The cavern was quiet except for the hum of eleven thousand clouds slowly forgetting how to exist.

Veyra opened her eyes. She looked at Pip. She looked at the jar in her claws.

"What does your rain cloud sound like?" she asked softly.

Pip's voice was barely a whisper. "Like this."

He rumbled — a low, gentle vibration that started in his belly and rolled outward, warm and steady as a lullaby. It was not the sound a dragon makes. It was the sound a sky makes when it is holding someone safe.

Veyra set the thunderhead jar on the floor. She uncorked it.

The tiny cloud rose, tentative, wobbling — and then it caught the air from the cavern entrance and *expanded*. It swelled to the size of a sheep, then a horse, then a carriage, darkening and brightening as it tumbled toward the open sky. A single crack of thunder rolled across the peaks, and Veyra felt it in her bones — not sad, not diminished, but *glad*.

"Oh," she breathed. "Oh, I see."

She turned to the shelf and reached for the amber jar — the sunset cloud, her newest, her pride. She pulled the cork.

The pink light spilled out like liquid, pouring across the cavern walls, pooling on the stone floor, and then rising — lifting — unfurling across the evening sky in ribbons of rose and gold that made Pip's breath catch and the mountain itself seem to glow.

"More," Pip whispered.

Veyra was already moving.

She pulled corks with her teeth, smashed jars with her tail, tipped vials from their shelves. Fog poured out in silver rivers. Cumulus clouds ballooned upward, brilliant white against the deepening blue. Cirrus clouds streaked across the sky like brushstrokes. Nimbus clouds rumbled and cracked and opened themselves wide, and the sky — the whole sky — erupted.

It rained. It thundered. It shimmered with light that had no name. The Stormspire Peaks, bare and gray for decades, were suddenly wreathed in color and mist and the wild, roaring music of a sky that had been held captive and was now, at last, free.

Veyra stood at the cavern entrance and let the rain soak her scales, let the wind push against her, let the clouds she had hoarded for so long rush past her like old friends leaving a party — happy, unhurried, gone.

And then she saw it.

Among the tumbling mass of released storm clouds, one small dark shape broke from the pack. It hovered uncertainly, then drifted toward Pip. It rumbled — soft, warm, unmistakable.

Pip froze. His eyes filled.

"You," he whispered. "I found you."

The cloud settled over him like a blanket, wrapping him in mist and the smell of rain on warm stone. Pip closed his eyes and leaned into it, and the rain cloud held him, and the mountain held them both, and Veyra watched and felt something she had not felt since she was a hatchling watching the sky on fire.

Not grief. Not fear.

*Enough.*

---

The jars stood empty. The shelves were bare. The cavern echoed with wind that smelled of rain and ozone and the green, growing scent of a world being watered.

Veyra ran her claws along the empty glass and smiled — a slow, strange, unfamiliar smile that made the last wisp of fog curl affectionately around her snout before it, too, drifted out into the open air.

She had spent a lifetime trying to keep beauty still, as though love were a thing you could cage. But beauty was not a jewel to be hoarded. It was a cloud — alive, moving, becoming. You did not hold it. You watched it. You let it go. And if you were lucky — if you were very, very lucky — it rained on you on the way out.

Below the Stormspire, the lowlands were drinking deep. Rivers swelled. Fields turned green. Children in distant villages looked up at the impossible sky — painted in every color, stitched with lightning, alive with thunder — and pointed and laughed and did not look away.

Veyra settled at the edge of the mountain, her empty jars catching starlight behind her, and watched the last of her clouds disappear over the horizon.

"Not goodbye," she murmured. "Just... elsewhere."

The wind, which had always loved this mountain, curled against her cheek and stayed.
