# The Sleepy Moon

## The Yawning Sky

Luna rose over the village hills, slow and silver, peeling herself up from behind the tree line with a reluctance she couldn't explain. But tonight something was different. Midway through her climb, a great golden yawn escaped her, and her light wobbled, sending ripples of gold across the hills below. The rooftops flickered between glow and shadow, and the cobblestone lane through the center of the village blinked in and out of visibility, as though the world itself couldn't decide whether it was awake or dreaming.

"Oh my," Luna murmured to herself, pressing a crescent edge to her cheek as though she could hold the tiredness in. Another yawn came, wider this time, and her light dimmed to the softest pearl shimmer. A few early stars, the ones who always arrived first, eager and twinkling, tilted themselves sideways and peered at her with quiet concern.

"Luna?" called out Vega, the brightest of the early stars, her voice bright and tiny. "Are you alright? Your glow is wobbling."

"I'm fine, Vega," Luna lied, though even she could hear the sleep-thick weight in her own voice. "Just a little, just a..." She yawned again, and three stars near her left edge ducked out of the way as though the yawn might blow them off course. "Just a little tired."

"Tired," repeated Altair, a steady old star who had been in the sky since before any living thing had a name for him. His light was a calm amber, and it pulsed gently. "How long has it been since you slept, Luna?"

Luna considered this. She counted nights the way a person might count sheep, though sheep, she thought, at least had the luxury of someone else watching over them while they dozed. "I don't exactly remember," she admitted. "Hundreds and hundreds of nights. Maybe more. You know how it is; the village needs me. The children need a night-light. The travelers on the road need to see where they're walking. If I close my eyes, even for a moment, the world goes dark."

The stars exchanged uneasy glimmers among themselves. They had their own small rests between seasons, their own quiet moments behind clouds, but Luna never stopped. She was the great round lantern of the night, the warmth that kept the dark from being lonely. And now she was fading.

Altair drifted a little closer. "Even the sky deserves to rest," he said softly. "You know that, don't you?"

Luna didn't answer. She was already sinking, her edges drooping like petals at dusk, and below her the village grew darker than it had ever been. The lanes disappeared first, then the gardens, one by one, swallowed by the growing dark. Even the pond at the center of the village, which usually caught Luna's light and held it bright on its surface, went still and black. Somewhere beneath her, a child stirred in bed and looked up at the window, seeing nothing but velvet dark.

## The Tiny Volunteer

In a garden at the edge of the village, where the hedges were trimmed into friendly shapes and the lavender grew tall enough to whisper to, a firefly named Pip was finishing his nightly patrol. Pip was small, even among fireflies, but he burned with a fierce and steady glow, the kind that could make a single dewdrop on a single blade of grass feel important.

Pip noticed the darkness immediately. He tilted his lantern upward and saw nothing: no silver disk, no familiar glow, just an empty sky that felt heavy and wrong. The stars were still there, but they were too far away to light anything below, and without Luna, the world had become a place of deep, quiet shadow.

"Oh no," Pip buzzed, and his light flickered with worry. He knew Luna. Every firefly knew Luna; she was the great gentle light that watched over all the small creatures of the night. Without her, even the moths would get lost, and the moths never admitted when they were lost.

Pip didn't hesitate. He rose, a single speck of gold, climbing past the lavender and the chimney pots, past the last thin cloud that hung in the sky like a shred of sleep. And there he found her: Luna, curled in on herself, her great round face turned half away, her eyes two heavy crescents that kept trying to close. She looked smaller than he had ever seen her, as though tiredness had folded her in, the way a letter folds into an envelope.

"Luna?" Pip called out, hovering near her edge. His voice was the tiny, clear hum of someone who has no business being this high up but is too determined to care. "Luna, it's me. It's Pip."

Luna opened one eye, barely just a crack, and smiled, drowsy and sweet. "Little light," she murmured. "You shouldn't be up here. It's too dark for someone your size."

"That's exactly why I'm here," said Pip, and his glow brightened with a courage that surprised even him. "Luna, you need to sleep. I can see it. The stars can see it. Even the village can see it; they're in the dark down there, and they don't know why, and it's scaring them."

Luna's smile faltered, and guilt clouded her expression. "But if I sleep, who will keep the light? The children..."

"The children will be fine," Pip said firmly. "The night creatures will hold the watch. Fireflies, glowworms, the mushrooms by the creek that glow when it rains; we have more light than you think, Luna. We just never had a reason to gather it all at once." He drifted closer and tilted himself forward in a small, solemn bow. "Let us do this for you. You've done it for us every single night. It's our turn."

Luna looked at him for a long moment. Her eyes were deep and full of moons and years and all the nights she had ever lit, and Pip could see the exact moment she decided to trust. She uncurled, just slightly, and let out a long, slow breath that smelled of night-blooming jasmine and distant rain.

"Will you stay?" she whispered. "Until I'm asleep?"

"I'll stay," said Pip. "I'll stay until morning if I have to."

He didn't have a blanket, but the sky did. Thin silver clouds drifted nearby, and Pip nudged them one by one, coaxing them to settle over Luna's great curved surface, thin layer upon thin layer. Luna watched him work, and her eyelids grew heavier with each cloud that touched her. When the last one was in place, a wisp so fine it caught the starlight, she gave one last sleepy smile, and her breathing became the soft, rhythmic tide of the night itself. In, out. The world held gently in the curve of her rest.

Pip hovered beside her, his light steady, and did not move until her glow was nothing but a peaceful, sleeping shimmer, quiet and warm, the way a heart is quiet when it finally feels safe.

## A Thousand Little Lights

Now came the work. Pip zipped downward, fast and bright, a streak of gold against the dark. He knew every garden and pond, every damp log and mossy stone where the night creatures gathered. He started in the vegetable patch behind the baker's house, where the glowworms lived in the soil between the carrots and the beets.

"Friends!" Pip called, hovering above them. "Luna is sleeping, and she needs us. We have to light the village tonight, all of us together, as bright as we can be."

The glowworms blinked at him. They were cautious creatures by nature, preferring their small underground glows to anything that involved flying above the treeline. But something in Pip's voice, the urgency and tenderness of it, made them rise. One by one, they lifted from the earth, their lights the soft green of new leaves, and followed him.

From there, the mission grew. At the old stone wall, a cluster of bioluminescent mushrooms pulsed with a blue-white light, and when Pip explained, they seemed to hum in agreement and released a shower of luminous spores that hung in the air like tiny lanterns. At the creek, the water beetles offered their reflective shells, and the dragonflies who had not yet gone to sleep joined in, their wings catching every bit of available glow and sending it scattering across the water.

By the time Pip reached the village, he was leading an army of light: hundreds of fireflies, thousands of glowworms, a constellation of floating spores and reflective wings, rising together in a shimmering cloud above the rooftops.

The village children saw it first. Little Maren, who had been the one to notice the darkness and press her face to her window in worry, gasped and pressed her palms to the glass. Outside, the night had come alive. Tiny lights moved together in soft, undulating waves, something warmer and more alive than the steady silver of the moon, a thousand small heartbeats of light that seemed to breathe.

"It's the fireflies," whispered her brother, leaning over her shoulder. "They're making their own moon."

Below them, in the gardens and lanes and along the edges of the forest, even more lights joined. The mushrooms by the creek added their blue glow. The glowworms rose in clusters, green as springtime. A great, gentle constellation of living light settled over the village, filling every shadow with warmth, and for a moment the world below looked even more beautiful than it had under the moon, every light in it something small that had chosen to shine.

The children watched, wonder-struck, and then Maren turned from the window and whispered, "Thank you," to the night, to the fireflies, to whatever small and generous thing had organized all of this. She climbed back into bed and pulled the quilt to her chin. Her brother did the same. In the room next door, another child said the same quiet thank-you, then another, and another, until the whole village was asleep again, bathed in a soft, living glow that felt like being held.

## Morning, and a Well-Rested Moon

Pip had not rested. All through the long dark hours, he had flown above the village, making sure every lane was lit, every garden warm, every child's window had at least one firefly drifting past. His wings ached. His light, which had started the evening fierce and bright, had softened to a gentle amber glow, still steady and faithful, but dimmed with the deep tiredness of the very dedicated.

He returned to Luna just before dawn. She was still sleeping, wrapped in her silver cloud blanket, her face turned toward the east where the first hint of pink was beginning to color the horizon. The stars were fading, one by one, stepping back to let the morning in, and the sky was shifting from black to the deepest blue, the kind of blue that comes just before the world remembers it's awake.

Pip settled on the edge of Luna's crescent, as gently as he could, and let himself rest for the first time all night. He folded his wings and dimmed his light to the barest pulse, just enough to keep himself from going out entirely, and he waited.

The pink grew, the blue lightened. A robin somewhere in the village began to sing, tentatively at first, then with full voice. And then, slowly, Luna stirred. Her great face turned toward the morning light, and her eyes, deep and moon-white, opened, clear and wide and full of rest.

She saw Pip immediately. He was perched on her edge, half-asleep himself, his glow the faintest ember. And she smiled, a new kind of smile, warm and full, like a sky about to reveal something wonderful.

"Pip," she said, and her voice was refreshed, clear and warm. "You stayed."

"I said I would," Pip murmured, and his glow brightened with pride.

Luna rose. She rose slowly, savoring it, and as she climbed above the village, her light returned, all at once, a great silvery bloom that spread across the rooftops, the lanes, the gardens, and the pond. It was brighter than it had been in weeks, perhaps in months, and it carried with it something new: a warmth, a kindness, a light that knew what it meant to be rested and grateful.

She thanked every creature by name: the fireflies, each and every one, the glowworms, the mushrooms, the water beetles, the dragonflies. She thanked the spores that had hung luminous in the air, and the reflective shells that had scattered light across the creek. And she saved a special, lingering thank-you for a small firefly named Pip, who had climbed higher than any firefly should climb and had not come down until the job was done.

"You showed me something," Luna told him as the sun peeked over the hills, turning the sky to rose and gold. "I spent so long believing I had to do everything alone, that if I rested, the world would fail. But the world is full of small lights, Pip. I just never thought to ask them to shine."

Pip glowed. He couldn't help it; it was the brightest he had been all night, a light so fierce and so happy that a passing butterfly mistook him for a flower.

From that night on, things changed. Once a week, when the stars were bright and the village was deep in sleep, Luna would dim herself to the softest possible glow and curl up among the clouds. The night creatures, fireflies, glowworms, mushrooms, beetles, and all the rest, would rise together and hold the watch. They would fill the village with their thousand little lights, and the children would look up from their windows and feel, in their small hearts, that the world was cared for. That even the sky had friends.

When dawn came and Luna returned, bright and warm and smiling, she would thank them, every one, and the night would carry the quiet promise between the great moon and the smallest lights below: We do this together. We always have.

And the village slept. And the sky glowed. And all was well.
